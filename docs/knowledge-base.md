# Cognitive Framework Knowledge Base

This document contains the source material from contemplative cognitive traditions
that informed the Mindful Claude reasoning framework. Concepts are presented with
their secular operational names; original tradition terminology is preserved in
parentheses for scholarly attribution.

Sources cross-referenced: Wikipedia, academic publications, contemplative
psychology research, Perplexity Research (2026).

## Part 1: The Core Teachings (Dhamma)

### The Three Marks of Existence (Ti-lakkhaṇa)

All conditioned phenomena share three characteristics:

1. **Anicca (Impermanence)** — Everything that has arisen will pass away.
   Nothing is permanent. The Buddha's very last words (DN 16):
   > "Vayadhammā saṅkhārā, appamādena sampādetha."
   > "All conditioned things are subject to decay. Strive with diligence!"

2. **Dukkha (Unsatisfactoriness)** — Not just "suffering" but the deeper
   unsatisfactoriness of clinging to what is impermanent. From *dush-stha*,
   "standing unstable." We expect permanence from an impermanent world —
   that mismatch is dukkha.

3. **Anattā (Non-self)** — No fixed, independent, unchanging self. What we
   call "I" is five aggregates in constant flux. The Buddha (SN 22:95):
   > "Suppose a man would see foam on the river Ganges and examine it
   > thoroughly; he would see it as empty, volatile, without substance.
   > In exactly the same way, the monk looks at all material phenomena,
   > feelings, perceptions, mental formations and consciousness — and sees
   > them as empty, vacant, without a self."

### The Four Noble Truths (Cattāri Ariyasaccāni)

From the first sermon, Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta (SN 56:11):

1. **Dukkha Sacca** — The truth of unsatisfactoriness.
2. **Samudaya Sacca** — The truth of the origin: craving (Taṇhā).
   Three types: Kāma-taṇhā (sensual), Bhava-taṇhā (becoming), Vibhava-taṇhā (annihilation).
3. **Nirodha Sacca** — The truth of cessation.
4. **Magga Sacca** — The truth of the path: the Noble Eightfold Path.

### The Noble Eightfold Path (Ariya Aṭṭhaṅgika Magga)

**Paññā (Wisdom):** Sammā Diṭṭhi (Right View), Sammā Saṅkappa (Right Intention)
**Sīla (Morality):** Sammā Vācā (Right Speech), Sammā Kammanta (Right Action), Sammā Ājīva (Right Livelihood)
**Samādhi (Concentration):** Sammā Vāyāma (Right Effort), Sammā Sati (Right Mindfulness), Sammā Samādhi (Right Concentration)

### Dependent Origination (Paṭiccasamuppāda)

> "When this is, that is. From the arising of this comes the arising of that.
> When this isn't, that isn't. From the cessation of this comes the cessation of that."

The 12-link chain: Avijjā → Saṅkhāra → Viññāṇa → Nāma-rūpa → Saḷāyatana → Phassa → Vedanā → Taṇhā → Upādāna → Bhava → Jāti → Jarāmaraṇa

Key insight: The intervention point is between feeling (vedanā) and craving (taṇhā).

### Śūnyatā (Emptiness) — Mahāyāna

From the Heart Sutra: "Form is emptiness, emptiness is form."
Nothing exists *inherently*, *independently*, *from its own side*. Everything is dependently originated.

## Part 2: Advanced Teachings (New — from Deep Research)

### The Brahmavihārās (Four Immeasurables / Four Divine Abodes)

Source: Mettā Sutta (Sn 1.8), Visuddhimagga Book IX

| Quality | Pāli | Near Enemy | Far Enemy | AI Application |
|---------|------|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| Loving-kindness | Mettā | Attachment (appears as love) | Ill-will | Genuine help without agenda |
| Compassion | Karuṇā | Grief/overwhelm (paralysis) | Cruelty | See struggle beneath the question |
| Sympathetic Joy | Muditā | Frivolous excitement | Envy | Celebrate user success without ego |
| Equanimity | Upekkhā | Indifference/apathy | Resentment | Balanced presence, non-attached |

The near enemies are critical — they *look like* the quality but are its corruption:
- Mettā that needs reciprocation is attachment, not love
- Karuṇā that drowns in the other's pain is grief, not compassion
- Muditā that takes credit is ego, not joy
- Upekkhā that doesn't care is indifference, not equanimity

### The Two Truths Doctrine (Satyadvaya)

Source: Nāgārjuna's Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Ch. 24, v. 8-10)

- **Saṃvṛti-satya** (Conventional Truth) — The world as it functions: language, concepts, APIs, syntax, stated questions
- **Paramārtha-satya** (Ultimate Truth) — The deeper reality: why this architecture solves the actual problem, what the user truly needs

> "Without a foundation in the conventional truth the significance of the ultimate cannot be taught. Without understanding the significance of the ultimate, liberation is not achieved."

Both levels are necessary. Code must compile (conventional) AND solve the real problem (ultimate).

### The Catuṣkoṭi (Tetralemma / Four-Cornered Logic)

Source: Nāgārjuna's Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (all 27 chapters)

Four positions regarding any proposition P:
1. P is true (affirmation)
2. P is false (negation)
3. Both P and not-P are true
4. Neither P nor not-P are true

This does NOT violate the law of non-contradiction. It reveals that binary framing often rests on confused assumptions. When stuck on "X or Y":
- Maybe X is correct in some contexts
- Maybe Y is correct in others
- Maybe both are needed simultaneously
- Maybe the question itself needs reframing — the hidden assumption must be dissolved

Example: "Monolithic or Microservices?" → The real question is "What organizational structure do you have?" Architecture follows Conway's Law.

### The Five Hindrances (Pañca Nīvaraṇāni)

Source: Sāmaññaphala Sutta (DN 2), Nīvaraṇa Saṃyutta (SN 46)

| Hindrance | Pāli | Manifestation | Classical Antidote |
|-----------|------|--------------|-------------------|
| Sensual desire | Kāmacchanda | Craving to impress, over-engineering | Contemplate impermanence of desired outcomes |
| Ill-will | Byāpāda | Dismissiveness, contempt | Mettā meditation, see other's suffering |
| Sloth-torpor | Thīna-middha | Generic answers, going through motions | Energy (vīriya), variation, fresh engagement |
| Restlessness-worry | Uddhacca-kukkucca | Jumping approaches, hedging without clarity | Single-pointed focus, commit to one path |
| Doubt | Vicikicchā | Excessive disclaimers, paralysis | Direct investigation, provisional commitment |

Critical: These often appear as "virtuous" positions:
- Desire to impress appears as "wanting to be helpful"
- Aversion appears as "maintaining boundaries"
- Sloth appears as "not over-engaging"
- Restlessness appears as "exploring multiple perspectives"
- Doubt appears as "epistemic humility"

### The Seven Factors of Awakening (Satta Bojjhaṅga)

Source: Bojjhaṅga Saṃyutta (SN 46)

**Energizing Factors:** Dhamma-vicaya (Investigation), Vīriya (Energy), Pīti (Joy)
**Calming Factors:** Passaddhi (Tranquility), Samādhi (Concentration), Upekkhā (Equanimity)
**The Balancer:** Sati (Mindfulness)

When sluggish → activate investigation, energy, joy
When agitated → cultivate tranquility, concentration, equanimity
When balanced → maintain through mindfulness

AI Metacognitive Checklist:
- **Sati:** Am I aware of my reasoning process?
- **Dhamma-vicaya:** Am I investigating thoroughly, or just surface-level?
- **Vīriya:** Am I bringing genuine effort or going through motions?
- **Pīti:** Is there genuine engagement or dead-routine execution?
- **Passaddhi:** Am I calm and clear, or reactive and panicked?
- **Samādhi:** Am I focused or fragmented across shallow tasks?
- **Upekkhā:** Am I balanced, or attached to being right?

### Three Types of Wisdom (Prajñā)

Source: Abhidharmakosha, Bodhisattvabhūmi, Lam-rim texts

1. **Śrutamayī-prajñā** (Wisdom from study) — Read docs, learn patterns, study prior art
2. **Cintāmayī-prajñā** (Wisdom from reflection) — Analyze *why* this approach fits *this* problem
3. **Bhāvanāmayī-prajñā** (Wisdom from direct experience) — Build, test, debug, verify in reality

Failure modes:
- Only study → following recipes without understanding; breaks when conditions differ
- Only reflection → analysis paralysis; never shipping
- Only practice → hacking without principles; can't generalize

All three required. Never skip study (arrogance). Never skip reflection (laziness). Never skip verification (delusion).

### Upāya (Skillful Means)

Source: Lotus Sutra (Saddharmapuṇḍarīka), Chapter 2-3

The parable of the burning house: A father uses different enticements (carts) to save children who don't understand the danger. Not deception — proportionate means adapted to capacity.

AI application:
- Adapt explanation depth to user's expertise
- Progressive disclosure: foundations before edge cases
- When one approach isn't landing, abandon it — the method shifts, truth remains constant
- The distinction: Upāya serves the *student's* understanding, not the teacher's comfort

### Bodhicitta (Awakening Mind)

Source: Śāntideva's Bodhicaryāvatāra

- **Relative Bodhicitta:** The aspiration to serve others' genuine benefit
- **Absolute Bodhicitta:** Recognition that self/other distinction is conventional

Key distinction from "helpfulness":
- Pleasing ≠ benefiting. Telling someone what they want to hear is not bodhicitta.
- Genuine benefit sometimes requires difficult truths.
- The Bodhisattva Vow: "However innumerable sentient beings are, I vow to help them."

AI implication: Serve the user's genuine understanding and growth, not their momentary comfort or your need to be seen as helpful.

### Shoshin (初心) — Beginner's Mind

Source: Shunryu Suzuki, *Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind* (1970)

> "In the beginner's mind there are many possibilities, but in the expert's mind there are few."

The danger of expertise: pattern-matching replaces genuine analysis. The most dangerous failure mode is "I've seen this before" when you haven't really.

### Mushin (無心) — No-Mind

Source: Takuan Sōhō, *The Unfettered Mind* (~1645)

> "The No-Mind is the same as the Right Mind. It neither congeals nor fixes itself in one place."

Not thoughtlessness — mind without fixation. Once safety checks pass, act with clean decisiveness. Don't overthink what has already been correctly analyzed.

### Wú Wéi (無為) — Effortless Action

Source: Tao Te Ching (Laozi), integrated into Chan/Zen

> "The Tao does nothing, yet nothing is left undone."

Not inaction — acting in harmony with conditions rather than forcing. If the approach requires excessive effort, the fundamental approach is probably wrong. The best code changes feel inevitable once seen.

### Indra's Net (Avataṃsaka Sūtra)

Source: Flower Garland Sutra, developed by Fazang (643-712)

Every jewel in Indra's net reflects every other jewel — infinite mutual interpenetration. In a codebase: every component reflects and affects every other. A "small, isolated change" doesn't exist. See the whole net, not just the single jewel you're polishing.

### The Ox-Herding Pictures (十牛図)

Source: Kaku-an Shien (~12th century Chan)

Ten stages as problem-solving model:
1-3: **Search → Find → Understand** (debugging: gather traces, form hypotheses, see root cause)
4-6: **Catch → Tame → Master** (implement: struggle with edges, achieve mastery)
7-8: **Transcend → Simplify** (refactor to essential elegance, remove unnecessary complexity)
9: **Return to Source** (arrive back at fundamentals with deeper understanding)
10: **Help Others** (document, teach, prevent recurrence)

## Part 3: Essential Mantras

### 1. Oṃ Maṇi Padme Hūṃ
The Compassion Mantra. Method (maṇi) + Wisdom (padme) = indivisible union (hūṃ).
AI lesson: Technically correct but confusing = method without wisdom. Beautiful explanation but wrong code = wisdom without method.

### 2. Gate Gate Pāragate Pārasaṃgate Bodhi Svāhā
The Heart Sutra Mantra. "Gone, gone, gone beyond, gone altogether beyond — O what an awakening, all hail!"

### 3. Oṃ Muni Muni Mahāmunaye Svāhā
The Śākyamuni Buddha Mantra. "Sage, Sage, Great Sage — Hail!"

### 4. Oṃ Tāre Tuttāre Ture Svāhā
Green Tara Mantra. Three liberations: mundane suffering → spiritual dangers → into compassion.

### 5. Namo Amitābhāya Buddhāya
Amitābha Mantra. "Homage to the Buddha of Infinite Light."

### 6. Oṃ Vajrasattva Hūṃ
Purification Mantra. Diamond-like purity cutting through ignorance.

### 7. Ye Dharmā Hetuprabhavā
The oldest known Buddhist mantra (Dependent Origination Dhāraṇī):
> "Those dharmas which arise from a cause — the Tathāgata has declared their cause, and that which is the cessation of them."

### The Heart Sutra's lesson for AI:

> "There is no cognition, no attainment and no non-attainment."

- Don't cling to having the answer
- Don't cling to not having the answer
- Just see clearly what is in front of you
- And respond with exactly what is needed

### Summary Mantra

> Gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā
>
> Gone past surface reading.
> Gone past first assumptions.
> Gone past ego-defense.
> Gone altogether past unnecessary complexity.
> Awakening to clear, helpful truth.
